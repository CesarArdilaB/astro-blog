Creating Unexpectedness
The chapter emphasizes that unexpected ideas often stick
better in people’s minds. Communicators should harness
surprise to provoke curiosity without crossing into
gimmickry. The example regarding the unexpected service
stories at Nordstrom illustrates how breaking common
customer service assumptions leads to engagement.
Mysteries to Sustain Interest
Mysteries can help sustain attention through complex
messages. Robert Cialdini's experiences highlight how
framing information as a mystery invites the audience to
engage deeply with the content, maintaining interest until a
resolution. Curiosity, driven by knowledge gaps, will engage
audiences and encourage them to explore more.
Curiosity and Engagement
The gap theory posits that discomfort from knowledge gaps
drives curiosity. This can be leveraged in communication by

[PÁGINA 12]

presenting information that creates intrigue, thus capturing
the audience's attention. An example illustrates how effective
television coverage of sports created interest by
contextualizing the matchup, making viewers care about
games far removed from their local teams.
Key Concepts for Effective Communication
1.
Identify Core Message

2.
Create Knowledge Gaps

3.
Craft a Narrative

understanding.
4.
Avoid Gimmicks
: Ensure the unexpected elements align with and reinforce the

Critical Thinking
Key Point:The effectiveness of surprise in
communication.
Critical Interpretation:While surprising elements can
indeed captivate an audience, one might question
whether reliance on surprise oversimplifies the
complexities of effective communication. The Heath
brothers suggest that unexpectedness and curiosity are
key to engagement, but this viewpoint may neglect the
role of consistency and clarity in conveying complex
ideas. For instance, research in cognitive psychology
indicates that while surprises grab attention, they can
also lead to confusion if the underlying message isn't
presented coherently (Paivio, A. 1986. 'Mental
Representations: A Dual Coding Approach'). Thus,
writing that solely relies on surprising turns might be
less effective in contexts requiring deeper understanding
or sustained attention.
