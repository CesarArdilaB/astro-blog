Chapter 25 | Monogamy Makes Words Mean| Q&A
1.Question
What does Mark Pollard mean by saying 'Monogamy
Makes Words Mean'?
Answer:Pollard suggests that words should have a
singular, strong meaning to be memorable and
impactful. Just like a monogamous relationship is
defined and exclusive, using words with clear,
distinct meanings ensures that the message captures
attention and resonates with others.
2.Question
How can strategists increase the meaning of their words?
Answer:By selecting words that convey specific and loud
meanings rather than using vague, big words. The clearer the
word's meaning, the more likely it is to stick in someone's
memory and communicate effectively.
3.Question
What are the dangers of using cacophonies in strategy?
Answer:Cacophonies—excessive and confusing
communication—can dilute meaning and lead to

[PÁGINA 443]

misunderstandings, making it hard for audiences to grasp the
core message. They can also arise from insecurity, a desire to
please everyone, or cultural pressures to produce more
content, which ultimately hinders effective communication.
4.Question
Why are words like 'pineapple' more memorable than
words like 'concepts'?
Answer:'Pineapple' has a specific, singular meaning that is
easily visualized and recognized, whereas 'concepts' can refer
to multiple ideas, which makes it less memorable. The study
mentioned emphasizes that words with one-to-one
relationships with their meanings are the most easily recalled.
5.Question
What should a strategist do when using big words?
Answer:Strategists should critically assess the big words they
choose, ensuring they serve a clear purpose. They should ask
if a simpler word might effectively communicate the same
idea without adding confusion. The goal is to maintain clarity
and ensure the core message resonates.
