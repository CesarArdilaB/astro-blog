Critical Thinking
Key Point:Selling is a fundamental, yet often
uncomfortable, aspect of strategic work.
Critical Interpretation:While Mark Pollard argues that
selling is an essential part of a strategist's role, one
might critically assess whether this view appropriately
captures the nuanced relationships within professional
environments. The pressure to sell can indeed dilute the
purity of intellectual work, as certain academics and
practitioners contend; for instance, scholars like Thomas
Piketty in 'Capital in the Twenty-First Century' suggest
that detachment can lead to greater insights devoid of
market influence. Thus, while Pollard's viewpoint
articulates a pervasive reality, it is worth considering
alternative perspectives that argue for intellectual
integrity without the necessity of selling.

Chapter 25 Summary : Monogamy
Makes Words Mean
Monogamy Makes Words Mean
Volume Control on Meaning
Strategists have the ability to amplify or diminish meaning
through their choice of words. Meaning resonates more when
it is specific and clear, standing out amid a clutter of
competing messages.
The Problem of Cacophony
Many strategists create noise instead of clarity due to:
- Fear of displeasing others by not including enough
information.
- Uncertainty about their own ideas, leading to distraction.
- Cultural norms that prioritize quantity over quality in
presentations.
- Lack of confidence to make decisive cuts in

[PÁGINA 113]

communication.
- A tendency to imitate competitors who overly fill their
presentations.
- Reliance on templates that demand excessive text.
The Power of Memorable Words
Memorable words, described as "monogamous," have a
single, strong meaning, making them easier to remember.
Research shows that words like "pineapple" are more easily
recalled than vague terms like "concern." Effective strategy
uses precise language rather than ambiguous or overly
complex words.
Guidelines for Effective Communication
While creativity, like wordplay, can be beneficial, clarity is
paramount. Strategists should avoid jargon and ensure that
any use of complex terms serves a clear purpose. The focus
should always be on a singular and clear idea that resonates
with the audience.
Conclusion

[PÁGINA 114]

To succeed, strategists must cultivate an infatuation with
concise writing that hones in on a clear, impactful message,
moving away from the noise of unnecessary complexity.
