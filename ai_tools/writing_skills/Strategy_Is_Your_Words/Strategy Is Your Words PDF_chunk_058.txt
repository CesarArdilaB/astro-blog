Types of Insights
There are different types of insights, each requiring careful
consideration:
-
Cultural Insights
: These emerge from observable public behaviors and
collective actions, often easier to formulate.
-
Individual Insights
: These focus on private feelings and thoughts, which may be
overlooked due to discomfort.

Challenges with Insights
Several factors complicate the formulation of insights:
1.
Lack of Clarity
: Many people don’t fundamentally understand what
constitutes an insight.
2.
View as Threat
: Insights can challenge existing beliefs, leading to resistance.
3.
Data Preference
: The dominance of numbers can overshadow the value of
insights, which are fueled by, but not equivalent to, data.
4.
Time-Consuming
: Developing true insights requires significant introspection
and life experience.
5.
Desire for Concrete
: Businesspeople often crave definitive insights, overlooking

[PÁGINA 194]

the nuanced and contextual nature of many insights.
6.
Artistic Element
: Insights involve intuition and creativity, which some may
overlook.
7.
Avoiding Mystique
: Some strategists elevate insights to an unapproachable
status, instead of sharing the process.
8.
Negative Attitudes
: Toxic dynamics, such as holier-than-thou attitudes or
undermining behaviors, hinder productive insight generation.
9.
Community Guilt
: The strategy community often wavers between valuing and
feeling guilty about their focus on insights.
10.
Confusing Language
: Overly complex or pretentious language dilutes the
meaning and importance of insights.
Conclusion

Appreciating the multifaceted nature and challenges of
insights allows strategists to engage with the process more
effectively, fostering a collaborative, clear, and
compassionate approach to understanding and generating
insights.

How Insights Work
Nature of Insights
Insights function as survival tools intrinsic to the human
experience. They articulate subconscious hunches, prompting
recognition and ownership of thoughts. By capturing
everyday behaviors and naming them, insights provide
meaning and structure, which are vital for human cognition
and behavior.
